Where are hormones produced?

Hormones act as messengers and carry signals about various biological functions to our cells. The main organs that make hormones include the pituitary, thyroid, hypothalamus, pancreas, and ovaries. Collectively, this system of organs forms the endocrine system.

Let’s break this down

Metabolic health

Focus: Sleep, Energy, Insulin Resistence

Metabolic health describes how well our body produces and uses energy. Every one of the trillions of cells in our bodies is a small powerplant that converts fuel (usually glucose or fat) to energy the body can use.

When our cells cannot run those processes efficiently, it can affect our sleep regulation, appetite, weight, and energy levels.

Reproductive health

Focus: Ovarian reserve, Sex drive

Hormones play the most significant role in our reproductive structures. They regulate the different stages of menstrual cycles and make conception possible while guiding puberty and menopause in women.

The most remarkable thing about reproductive hormones? They simultaneously guide our bodies, brains, and behavior toward the same reproduction-related goals.

Our story

WE BELIEVE Information is power
Hormones disproportionately affect women.

Women often experience hormonal imbalance at predictable and naturally occurring points in their lives (menstruation, puberty, pregnancy, and menopause).

On top of that, changing lifestyles and urban living give rise to Endocrine Disrupting Chemicals (EDCs). EDCs are found in our environment, food, and items we use each day, including beauty and cleaning products. These "estrogenic" chemicals can interrupt the endocrine function and confuse the body into over-or under-producing essential hormones.

So why don't we test for hormones in our regular health checkups?

Understanding your hormone profile allows you to learn more about your fertility, discover any undetected problems, and have the flexibility to create a plan based on your symptoms. A hormonal screening can detect an underlying imbalance, giving you the correct information to make decisions for your body.

For decades, women were often excluded from clinical trials and research studies.
  • -> Researchers assumed that male and female bodies were essentially the same - the vast majority of biological literature is based on single-sex studies of males of European ancestry.
  • -> Researchers worried about the fluctuations of hormones during the menstrual cycle.
  • -> Researchers feared that experimenting on reproductive-aged females would hurt their fertility (The assumption, of course, being that all people with ovaries automatically want to have kids.)

Historically, research looking into the health of people with ovaries, in general, has been underfunded. Many aspects of our metabolic and reproductive functions were unknown until the past few decades. (Not-so-fun fact: The complete anatomy of the clitoris wasn't discovered until 1998.)

What's more, there is a lack of representation of South Asian women in these studies. 25 years later, we are still playing catch up. This time, we don't intend to be left out.
